All consonants · vowels · nasal finals · tones

🔑 Key concept: Aspirated vs. Unaspirated

In each pair (b–p, d–t, g–k, j–q, zh–ch, z–c) the tongue position is exactly the same.
The only difference: the second sound has a strong puff of air (aspiration).

1. b p m f

b (ㄅ) [p]

Lips: Pressed together – block air completely, then release gently.
Airflow: Unaspirated (no strong puff).
Practice: 爸 (bà), 杯 (bēi)

p (ㄆ) [pʰ]

Lips: Same as b: press, then release.
Airflow: Strongly aspirated – more air pushed out.
Practice: 怕 (pà), 皮 (pí)

m (ㄇ) [m]

Lips: Closed (like b/p), but air escapes through the nose.
Practice: 妈 (mā), 门 (mén)

f (ㄈ) [f]

Mouth: Lower lip touches upper front teeth lightly; air flows continuously.
Practice: 发 (fā), 饭 (fàn)

👄 Group recap: b / p / m / f
  • b → lips, no puff
  • p → lips, strong puff
  • m → lips, nasal (air through nose)
  • f → lip + teeth, continuous airflow

2. d t n l

d (ㄉ) [t]

Tongue: Tip against the alveolar ridge (gums behind upper teeth).
Block air with tongue tip, then release gently (no puff).
Practice: 大 (dà), 灯 (dēng)

t (ㄊ) [tʰ]

Tongue: Same as d – tip on alveolar ridge.
Airflow: Strong aspiration (more air pushed out).
Practice: 他 (tā), 天 (tiān)

n (ㄋ) [n]

Tongue: Same alveolar position, but air goes through the nose.
Practice: 你 (nǐ), 男 (nán)

l (ㄌ) [l]

Tongue: Tip touches alveolar ridge, but air flows out over the sides of the tongue.
Practice: 了 (le), 来 (lái)

👅 Group recap: d / t / n / l
  • All four start with the tongue tip on the alveolar ridge.
  • d → block, release (no puff)
  • t → block, release with strong puff
  • n → block, nasal (air escapes through nose)
  • l → tip stays, air flows around sides (no blockage)

3. g k h

g (ㄍ) [k]

Tongue: Raise the back of the tongue against the soft palate.
Block air, then release gently (unaspirated).
Practice: 哥 (gē), 给 (gěi)

k (ㄎ) [kʰ]

Tongue: Same back-of-tongue position as g.
Airflow: Strong aspiration – a forceful release.
Practice: 可 (kě), 开 (kāi)

h (ㄏ) [x]

Tongue: Back raised close to soft palate, but no complete closure.
Air flows continuously – like a gentle rough breath.
Practice: 喝 (hē), 好 (hǎo)

🗣️ Group recap: g / k / h
  • All three raise the back of the tongue.
  • g → full closure, no puff
  • k → closure, strong puff
  • h → no closure, air flows freely

4. j q x

x (ㄒ) [ɕ]

Tongue: Tip behind lower front teeth. Front of tongue raises toward hard palate.
Mouth: Spread wide, like a smile. Air flows continuously – no blockage.
Practice: 西 (xī), 小 (xiǎo)

j (ㄐ) [tɕ]

Tongue: Same position as x. Press front of tongue against hard palate – block air, then release gently (unaspirated).
Practice: 鸡 (jī), 见 (jiàn)

q (ㄑ) [tɕʰ]

Tongue: Same as j (block then release).
Airflow: Strong aspiration – push out more air.
Practice: 七 (qī), 去 (qù)

🔁 j / q / x – the palatal family
  • Tongue tip down behind lower teeth.
  • x → air flows, no closure (like a hissing smile)
  • j → full closure then gentle release (no puff)
  • q → closure + strong burst of air

5. zh ch sh r

zh (ㄓ) [ʈʂ]

Tongue: Curl tip back to touch the hard palate just behind the alveolar ridge.
Block air, then release gently (unaspirated).
Practice: 知 (zhī), 中 (zhōng)

ch (ㄔ) [ʈʂʰ]

Tongue: Same curled position as zh.
Airflow: Strong aspiration – a forceful puff.
Practice: 吃 (chī), 车 (chē)

sh (ㄕ) [ʂ]

Tongue: Curl tip close to the same spot, but do not touch. Air flows continuously.
Practice: 师 (shī), 上 (shàng)

r (ㄖ) [ɻ]

Tongue: Same as sh (curled, close to palate).
Voice: Vibrate your vocal cords – the voiced partner of sh.
Practice: 日 (rì), 人 (rén)

🌀 zh / ch / sh / r – retroflex family
  • Tongue tip curled back toward the hard palate.
  • zh → tip touches, no puff
  • ch → tip touches, strong puff
  • sh → curled close, continuous airflow (voiceless)
  • r → same as sh, but with vocal cord vibration (voiced)

6. z c s

z (ㄗ) [ts]

Tongue: Tip touches the back of the upper front teeth.
Block air, then release gently (unaspirated).
Practice: 子 (zǐ), 早 (zǎo)

c (ㄘ) [tsʰ]

Tongue: Same dental position as z.
Airflow: Strong aspiration – a sharp puff of air.
Practice: 词 (cí), 草 (cǎo)

s (ㄙ) [s]

Tongue: Tip close to upper front teeth. Air flows continuously – no closure.
Practice: 四 (sì), 三 (sān)

🦷 z / c / s – dental sibilants
  • Tongue tip near the upper front teeth.
  • z → full contact, no puff
  • c → full contact, strong puff
  • s → close but not touching, continuous air

✅ Quick‑reference: all consonants

  • b [p] – lips, no puff
  • p [pʰ] – lips, strong puff
  • m [m] – lips, nasal
  • f [f] – lip+teeth
  • d [t] – alveolar, no puff
  • t [tʰ] – alveolar, strong puff
  • n [n] – alveolar, nasal
  • l [l] – alveolar, lateral
  • g [k] – soft palate, no puff
  • k [kʰ] – soft palate, strong puff
  • h [x] – soft palate, friction
  • j [tɕ] – palatal, no puff
  • q [tɕʰ] – palatal, strong puff
  • x [ɕ] – palatal, continuous
  • zh [ʈʂ] – retroflex, no puff
  • ch [ʈʂʰ] – retroflex, strong puff
  • sh [ʂ] – retroflex, voiceless
  • r [ɻ] – retroflex, voiced
  • z [ts] – dental, no puff
  • c [tsʰ] – dental, strong puff
  • s [s] – dental, continuous

7. Simple vowels: i, u, ü, a, o

i (ㄧ) [i] – high front unrounded

Mouth: Spread lips wide, tense. Tongue: High front, tip behind lower teeth.
Practice: 一 (yī), 衣服 (yīfu)

u (ㄨ) [u] – high back rounded

Mouth: Small circle, protrude slightly. Tongue: Pull back, raise back toward soft palate.
Practice: 五 (wǔ), 不 (bù)

ü (ㄩ) [y] – high front rounded

Tongue: Exactly like [i] (high front). Mouth: Round lips tightly – only difference from i.
Practice: 鱼 (yú), 去 (qù)

a (ㄚ) [a] – low central unrounded

Mouth: Open wide, jaw dropped. Tongue: Flat, low, central.
Practice: 妈 (mā), 大 (dà)

o (ㄛ) [o] – high‑mid back rounded

Mouth: Rounded, less tight than [u]. Tongue: Pull back, raise mid‑back.
Practice: 我 (wǒ), 多 (duō)

⚠️ Special rule: "o" after b, p, m, f

When o follows b, p, m, f alone, it sounds like uo [wo].
Example: 潑水 (pō shuǐ) → "puo", 模仿 (mó fǎng) → "muo".

8. Nasal finals: en/eng · in/ing · an/ang

Front nasal (-n)
Tip touches alveolar ridge

Back nasal (-ng)
Back of tongue raises to soft palate

FinalIPAHow to pronounceExample
en[ən]Start with schwa, then tip of tongue touches upper gums (front nasal).本 (běn)
eng[əŋ]Start with schwa, then back of tongue raises to soft palate (back nasal).风 (fēng)
in[in]Start with [i], then tip to alveolar ridge for -n.心 (xīn)
ing[iŋ]Start with [i], then raise back of tongue for -ng.请 (qǐng)
an[an]Start with open [a], then tip to alveolar ridge for -n.看 (kàn)
ang[ɑŋ]Start with back [ɑ] (wider mouth), then back tongue to soft palate.上 (shàng)
⚠️ Common mistake: -ian / -üan → -ien / -üen

When i- or ü- is followed by -an, the [a] shifts to [e]:

  • ian → i + en (先 xiān sounds like "shi‑en")
  • üan → ü + en (全 quán sounds like "chyü‑en")

9. Tones – the complete picture

🎵 The real secret: tones are relative, not absolute

The 1–5 scale is a relative reference, not a musical note. Tone 1 is not a strained high “55” – it’s a comfortable mid‑to‑high‑mid flat tone. Tone 3 is mostly just a low tone, not a full fall‑rise.

9.1 The third tone & tone‑sandhi

🔎 The real third tone

In everyday speech the third tone is usually just a low tone that dips slightly – often with a creaky voice. The full 2→1→4 contour only appears at the end of a phrase or for emphasis.

🔄 3‑3 → half‑2 + 3

When two third tones meet, the first becomes a half tone 2 (a short rise, not a full second tone):

  • 你好 (nǐ hǎo) → ní hǎo
  • 想法 (xiǎng fǎ) → xiáng fǎ
  • 雨傘 (yǔ sǎn) → yú sǎn
🚂 Sandhi trains (3+ third tones)

Within a word, every third tone except the last becomes tone 2. Across word boundaries the chain may break.

  • 管理組 → guán lí zǔ
  • 買雨傘 → mái yú sǎn (verb + object)

9.3 Connected speech: downdrift & assimilation

📉 Downdrift – the phrase curve

Over a sentence, the overall pitch drifts downward. A tone 1 at the end is lower than at the start. Pronouncing every syllable at textbook pitch sounds robotic.

Example: 媽媽罵馬 (māma mà mǎ) – the second “mā” and “mǎ” are lower than the first.

🔗 Carry‑over effects

A high‑ending tone raises the start of the next syllable; a low‑ending tone lowers it. This is natural smoothing – don’t force it, just listen and imitate.

9.4 Emotion & questions – without breaking tones

🌊 Shift your pitch range, not the tone shape

Surprise → raise whole range. Sadness → lower whole range. Anger → add extra fall at the very end. Never twist the contour of the tone itself.

❓ Questions – higher register, not a final rise

Raise the pitch of the whole question and exaggerate the final tone contour. Never add an English‑style rise.

Contrast: 這是水。 (normal) vs. 這是水？ (higher register, magnified low tone)

9.5 The neutral tone (輕聲)

⚡ Short, weak, borrows pitch from before
After tone…Neutral tone pitchExample

The neutral tone is an unstressed light beat. Make it short and light, and you’ll be understood.

🗣️ Quick practice – tones across emotions

  • 真的有？ – “Really?” – keep “有” low; whole voice higher.
  • 太好了！ – “Great!” – “好” stays low even when excited.
  • 不要問了！ – “Stop asking!” – tones intact, sharp final drop.
  • 這是水？ – “This is water?” – higher register; “shuǐ” magnified.
✅ Summary: Mandarin tones in one page
  1. Tone charts are relative. Tone 1 is mid‑high, tone 3 is a low tone – not a dramatic fall‑rise.
  2. Each tone has a duration & intensity profile: 4th is shortest, 3rd is longest and quietest.
  3. Downdrift gently lowers pitch across a sentence; without it you sound robotic.
  4. Tone sandhi (3‑3, 一, 不) are systematic – learn the patterns, then trust your ear.
  5. Chinese intonation exists – raise the whole pitch range for questions, never twist the tone contour.
  6. Assimilation smooths out transitions automatically; don’t force it.
  7. Aim for naturalness, not a mythical “perfect” pitch. Regional and individual variation is normal.
