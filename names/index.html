Chinese Names:
Surnames, History & How to Choose One

Every Chinese name carries centuries of history, philosophy, and family hopes. Let’s unpack it all.

📖 The building blocks

In China, the surname (姓 xìng) comes first, then the given name (名 míng). Most names are two or three characters: one for the surname and one or two for the given name. This order reflects the deep-rooted importance of the family over the individual.

Example

王 (Wáng) – surname
小明 (Xiǎomíng) – given name
→ Full name: 王小明 (Wáng Xiǎomíng)

Rare two‑character surnames (复姓) like 欧阳 (Ōuyáng) or 司马 (Sīmǎ) still exist.

🏺 How surnames came to be

Chinese surnames weren’t always like today. Thousands of years ago, during the time of the Yellow Emperor, names were passed down through the mother, not the father. The character for “surname” (姓) itself contains the radical for “woman” (女) – a fossil of that ancient matriarchal society.

The matriarchal beginning

In prehistory, noble families had two types of surnames: 姓 (xìng) from the mother and 氏 (shì) from the father. The xìng represented the larger tribe, while the shì was a branch within it. Two people with the same xìng could not marry – an early form of preventing inbreeding.

There were “Eight Great Surnames” of prehistory: 姬, 姜, 姒, 嬴, 妘, 妫, 姚, 姞. Notice they all contain the “woman” radical – a clear sign of the matriarchal era.

The shift to paternal surnames

During the Warring States period (475 BC), people started using their shì as their permanent surname. By the Han dynasty, the distinction between xìng and shì disappeared, and surnames were passed from father to child – the system we know today.

The Han dynasty book Fengsu Tongyi explains that all surnames come from nine sources: clan name, noble rank, state name, official position, style name, residence, event, occupation, or a granted name – strikingly similar to the origins of English surnames.

🔤 From 9 sources – Chinese vs. English

Rank: 王 (Wáng, “king”) – like “King”.
Occupation: 陶 (Táo, “potter”) – like “Potter”; 匠 (Jiàng, “craftsman”) – like “Smith”.
Official position: 司马 (Sīmǎ, “Minister of War”).
Place: 西门 (Xīmén, “West Gate”).

🏷️ The top 10 surnames today

The “Old Hundred Surnames” (老百姓 Lǎobǎixìng) is a term for common folk. Today, just 100 surnames cover 87% of the population. Here are the heavyweights.

王
Wáng
"King" – the most common surname in China (over 100 million). Originated from nobility; a name that commands respect.
李
Lǐ
"Plum" – second most common. Rose to prominence as the imperial surname of the Tang Dynasty.
张
Zhāng
"Archer" – originally bestowed on the grandson of the Yellow Emperor who invented the bow. The radical 弓 means "bow".
刘
Liú
An ancient surname meaning "kill" or "axe", carried by the Han Dynasty emperors.
陈
Chén
From the state of Chen; means "exhibit" or "old".
杨
Yáng
"Poplar tree" – associated with strength and endurance. Widely spread during the Sui Dynasty.
黄
Huáng
"Yellow" – once a noble clan from the ancient State of Huang.
赵
Zhào
From the State of Zhao. Listed first in the "Hundred Family Surnames" (百家姓) because the Song emperor’s name was Zhao.
吴
Wú
From the Kingdom of Wu – a prosperous coastal region in ancient times.
周
Zhōu
Refers to the mighty Zhou Dynasty, one of China’s longest‑lasting dynasties.

✨ The heart: given names

Given names are carefully crafted wishes. They can be poetic, aspirational, or balanced by the five elements. Let’s see how parents choose them.

Common themes

  • Virtues: 智 (wisdom), 勇 (bravery), 谦 (modest), 文 (cultured)
  • Beauty: 美 (beautiful), 芳 (fragrant), 婷 (graceful)
  • Strength: 伟 (great), 强 (strong), 杰 (outstanding)
  • Nature: 云 (cloud), 山 (mountain), 花 (flower), 明 (bright)
  • Joy & peace: 悦 (joy), 宁 (calm) – gender‑neutral and modern

Generation names (辈份)

In traditional families, siblings and cousins share one character from a generation poem. Every person in that generation has that same character in their name, creating a “secret family code”.

Example: The La family poem begins:
Uphold ancestral traditions… (承先启后)—the first character 承 (chéng) becomes the shared character for that generation: 承德 (Chéngdé, “carry virtue”) and 承芳 (Chéngfāng). The next generation uses 先 (xiān), and so on.

📅 Naming trends through the decades

Names reflect the spirit of their time. Here’s how Chinese given names evolved over the last 70 years.

1950s

建国 (Jiànguó) – "Build the Country"
Post‑revolution optimism; names celebrating the new nation.

1960s–70s

红 (Hóng) – "Red", 军 (Jūn) – "Army"
Cultural Revolution influences; revolutionary and military names.

1980s–90s

伟 (Wěi), 杰 (Jié), 雅 (Yǎ)
Personal qualities – greatness, elegance, and aspiration.

2000s–today

Unique double‑character names
Handcrafted and aesthetic combinations, sometimes whimsical, always meaningful.

Even pandas get in on the trend: the famous panda 震生 (Zhènshēng) was “born in an earthquake” in 2017.

🪷 The Five Elements & name balance

Metal, Wood, Water, Fire, Earth

Chinese metaphysics believes everyone has a balance of the five elements (五行 wǔxíng). If you’re too strong in one, your name can counteract it. For example, if you have too much metal, parents might choose characters with the wood radical (木), because metal “cuts” wood – a weakening effect to restore balance.

If a person is weak in all five elements, they might even be given a name that contains all five radicals in its characters – a super‑charged balancing act.

Extreme examples

Too much metal? Use 林 (lín, “forest”) – two wood radicals. Still not enough? Use 森 (sēn, “many trees”) – three wood radicals. There’s also the famous name with all five elements: 鑫淼焱垚 (a hypothetical name combining metal, water, fire, and earth radicals). People take this seriously – professional name‑givers still consult horoscopes.

📜 Beyond the given name

Courtesy names (字)

Historically, upon reaching adulthood, a person received a courtesy name. This was used in public; the given name was reserved for elders. For example, Confucius’s given name was 孔丘 (Kǒng Qiū), but he’s known as 孔子 (Kǒngzǐ) – the suffix 子 is an honorific for a great sage.

Emperors’ many names

Emperors juggled several names: a temple name (唐太宗 Táng Tàizōng), a posthumous name (汉武帝 Hàn Wǔdì – “Martial Emperor”), and an era name (康熙 Kāngxī). Don’t worry, ordinary people only had one or two.

🔡 Why is Zhang spelled Cheung or Chang?

Different romanization systems cause the same surname to appear as multiple spellings. 张 (Zhāng) in pinyin becomes Chang in Wade‑Giles, Cheung in Cantonese, and Chong in Hakka. If you see “John Chung”, it’s likely the same old 张. This applies to 王 (Wáng → Wong) and 李 (Lǐ → Lee) too.

🧧 How to choose your own Chinese name

1. Sound like you

Phonetic transliteration works: Tom → 汤姆 (Tāngmǔ), Anna → 安娜 (Ānnà). Simple and effective.

2. Pick meaningful characters

Choose words that express your values: 文 (Wén, cultured), 谦 (Qiān, modest), 悦 (Yuè, joy), 宁 (Níng, calm) – all gender‑neutral.

3. Watch gender & balance

While many modern names are unisex, characters like 婷 (tíng) lean feminine, and 磊 (lěi) masculine. Consider the five elements if you like metaphysics.

4. Ask a native speaker

Always run your chosen name by a native to avoid accidental homophones for “death” (死) or awkward combinations.

⚠️ Naming taboos

  • Don’t share a character with an elder – especially parents or grandparents. Even reading a character from a senior’s name aloud was once considered rude.
  • Avoid unlucky sounds – homophones for illness, death, or gloom are carefully sidestepped.
  • Balance the elements – as explained, a name can correct an elemental imbalance.
  • Rare characters – using too obscure a character can cause ID and computer issues; modern laws recommend common characters.
